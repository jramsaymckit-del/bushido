武 士 道

BUSHIDO

the way of daily discipline
道

Most people know what they want to become. Almost none of them have a reliable way to get there. Not because they lack ambition — but because ambition without structure evaporates. BUSHIDO was built to close that gap. To take the largest thing you can imagine for your life and connect it, without a single broken link, to what you do before breakfast.

Why this was built

The tools that exist for goal-setting and habit tracking are largely broken — not because they are poorly made, but because they treat the two things as separate. You set goals in one place. You track habits somewhere else. Neither knows about the other. The goal sits in a document, slowly losing relevance. The habit sits in an app, disconnected from any reason to do it.

BUSHIDO was built because that separation is the problem. Your morning run is not just a habit. It is an expression of who you have decided to be — a person who tends their body, who shows up for themselves before the world demands anything. When you see that clearly, the habit stops feeling like a chore and starts feeling like identity. Identity is far harder to abandon than a streak counter.

This app exists to make that connection visible — and to keep it visible, every day, for the rest of your life.

The philosophy — 武士道

Bushido, the way of the warrior, was the code that governed the samurai — not in battle, but in life. Honour. Discipline. Mastery. Loyalty. The samurai did not become extraordinary in the moment of the sword. He became extraordinary in ten thousand mornings of practice before it ever mattered.

That is the philosophy here. Not aggression. Not conquest. The warrior's code applied to the only battle that truly matters: the one you fight with yourself, every day, to become the person you are capable of being.

The Japanese concept of Ikigai asks: what is your reason for being? Shokunin is the artisan's devotion to craft — not for recognition, but for the love of doing it well. Kaizen is the commitment to getting 1% better, every day, without exception. These are not productivity techniques. They are a way of moving through the world. BUSHIDO borrows them because they are true — and because they work.

Why a cascade?

Most goal-setting fails because it lives only at the top — the vision — with nothing connecting it to today. You know where you want to be in ten years. You have no idea what to do at 7am on a Tuesday.

BUSHIDO solves this by building downward. Your identity (Ryughi) informs your annual intentions (Ikigai). Your intentions shape the crafts you sharpen (Shokunin). Your crafts demand daily practice (Kaizen). Every habit you check carries the full weight of your vision inside it. The small act is never small — it is the whole mountain in miniature.

What BUSHIDO is not

It is not a productivity tool. It is not a task manager. It is not about doing more. It is about doing the right things — the things that, repeated over years, make you the person you decided to become. The app does not make you disciplined. You make you disciplined. The app simply holds the record of whether you showed up.

There is no gamification here. No badges for streaks. No notifications pressuring you back. BUSHIDO does not chase you. It waits. When you come back — and you will come back — it shows you exactly where you are, without judgment, and asks only one thing: will you practise today?

The Four Pillars

流儀
Ryughi — Your Way
The strands of your identity
2–5 threads that define who you are and who you are becoming. Not goals — qualities. "Health & Vitality." "Creative Expression." "Family Bonds." These are the currents running beneath everything you do.
生き甲斐
Ikigai — Your Purpose
Vision, milestones, and this year's goals
Begin with a vivid 10-year vision. Then: what must be in motion at year five? What must this year accomplish? Ikigai gives your daily practice its reason for existing.
職人
Shokunin — Your Craft
The skills you are sharpening
The artisan does not rush toward mastery. Shokunin tracks your monthly and weekly crafts — running, writing, learning, building — each linked to an annual goal. The Shu-Ha-Ri scale marks your progress: Learn → Break → Transcend.
改善
Kaizen — Daily Practice
The small habits that compound
塵も積もれば山となる — even dust accumulates into mountains. Kaizen are your non-negotiable daily and weekly practices. Small enough to do even on bad days. Powerful enough to change everything over time.

Getting Started

1
Create your account
On your first visit, tap Create Account and enter your email and password. Your account is what links BUSHIDO to the cloud — sign in on any device and your scroll follows you.
2
Complete the onboarding meditation
After signing in you'll be guided through a meditation that builds your personal cascade — from Ryughi strands through to daily habits. Take your time. There are no wrong answers, only honest ones.
3
Open the app every day
The Kaizen tab is your home. Check off habits as you complete them. Write a line in the daily reflection. Come back tomorrow. That's the whole practice.
4
Reflect weekly and monthly
A prompt appears on Sunday evenings and during the last three days of each month. These reflections are where the real growth happens — reading your week back to yourself before writing about it.

BUSHIDO User Guide 02

改善   Kaizen

Your daily practice — the tab you open every morning.

塵も積もれば山となる

Even dust accumulates into mountains.

Kaizen does not mean dramatic change. It means small, consistent improvement — so small it feels almost invisible on any given day. A five-minute run. Ten pages read. One line written. These are the particles of dust. You are not trying to move a mountain. You are trying to show up, again, and let the dust settle where it lands.

The mountain is not the goal. The mountain is what accumulates — quietly, inevitably — when you stop trying to conquer it and simply practice it.

Consistency over intensity

The warrior who trains every day at 70% outperforms the warrior who trains once a week at 100%. BUSHIDO is not built for the days when you feel like a warrior. It is built for the days when you don't — and you show up anyway. That is the practice. That is the mountain being made.

Your habits are your identity expressed in action. Every time you check one off, you are casting a vote for the person you are becoming. Every time you don't, you are not failing — you are simply not voting. Come back tomorrow and vote again.

Other Features Worth Knowing

道
The 道 Seal — Stats Easter Egg
Scroll to the very bottom of the scroll. Click the faint 道 seal. Your practice statistics unfold — current streak, personal best, lifetime sessions, and 30-day completion rate for every habit. Grouped by craft. A quiet archive of how far you've come.
☽
Dark Mode
After 9PM the parchment scroll shifts to warm charcoal automatically. The red accents stay. Everything else inverts. Your evening practice is easier on the eyes. Reverts to daylight at 6AM. No setting needed.
☁
Cloud Sync — Automatic
Your data saves to the cloud on every habit check and edit. Sign into your account on any device — phone, laptop, anywhere — and your scroll is exactly where you left it. No setup required.
↓
Export & Import
The ↓ export button saves your entire scroll as a dated JSON backup. Store it somewhere safe. If you ever need to restore — new phone, new browser — ↑ import loads it back instantly. Never lose your practice history.

A note on streaks

If you forget to check off a habit and fill it in the next day using the week grid, your streak is preserved. The app reads your actual completion history, not just what you tapped today. Retroactive fills count.

Celebrations

Complete all of today's habits and the 道 seal at the bottom animates. Complete a full Mon–Sun week and the proverb 塵も積もれば山となる rises across the screen. Complete a month at 90%+ and the scroll glows red with your exact percentage. These are earned, not given.

氏   My Clan

The accountability tab — your practice, witnessed.

Tap the 氏 MY CLAN tab at the top to enter the clan view. Here you see the McKitrick Clan's collective practice — real data, updated live every time a member checks a habit.

侍
Samurai Mode
Your clan sees your full scroll — strands, crafts, annual goals with progress, strand completion rates, and today's habit count. Nothing hidden. The warrior who shows up fully earns the right to be seen.
忍
Shinobi Mode
Toggle to Shinobi and your clan sees only your kanji embers and streak dots — completion patterns without detail. Your path is visible. Your plan is yours alone.
氏
Clan Momentum Box
The top card shows your clan name, warrior count, this week's aggregate completion rate, today's clan score, and a 7-day bar chart averaging everyone's daily practice. You rise and fall together.
武
Active Warriors
Below the momentum box, each clan member gets a card showing their strands, crafts, goals, completion rates, streak, and today's count — or just their kanji embers if they're in Shinobi mode. Data updates every time they check a habit.

年   Year-End Review

The annual closure ritual — how the warrior ends a year.

At year's end, the 道 seal at the bottom of your scroll glows to signal it is time. The Year-End Review is a four-phase ritual — not a report card, but a closing ceremony for the year that was.

I
Gate — The Numbers
Your year in statistics. Daily, weekly, and monthly habit counts. Completion rates by strand. A sealed view of your Ikigai. The gate does not judge — it simply tells the truth.
II
Read Your Year
Every reflection you wrote — daily notes, weekly entries, monthly moments — surfaces in full. Filtered by strand or habit type. You read your year before you judge it.
III
Reflect — Strand by Strand
For each of your Ryughi strands: a reflection prompt, a verdict (Advancing / Holding / Falling), and space to write what you noticed. Closes with one question for the whole year: what did this year teach you?
IV
Seal — The 印
Rate each strand, read back your year's lesson, and press the 印 seal. The year is closed. Your notes are preserved. The scroll is ready for what comes next.

Appendix — Japanese Symbols

Every character used in BUSHIDO, with its reading and meaning.

Core Philosophy

武士道 Bushido The way of the warrior. The name of the app and the philosophy behind it — discipline, integrity, and purpose practised daily.
道 Do / Michi The Way or Path. Appears on the hanko seal, the My Bushido navigation tab, and the 365-day milestone stamp. The character you are always walking toward.
氏 Uji / Shi Clan or Family. Appears on the My Clan navigation tab. The people who walk the path alongside you.

The Four Pillars

流儀 Ryughi Your unique way or style. 流 (Ryu) means flow; 儀 (Gi) means ceremony or manner. Your Ryughi is the refined, intentional way you move through life — the through-line of everything else.
生き甲斐 Ikigai Reason for being. 生き (Iki) means life or living; 甲斐 (Gai) means worth or result. Your long-horizon goals — the 5 and 1-year milestones that give the practice its direction.
職人 Shokunin The artisan or craftsperson. 職 (Shoku) means occupation; 人 (Nin) means person. Your crafts — the specific skills and practices you are mastering in service of your goals.
改善 Kaizen Continuous improvement. 改 (Kai) means change or reform; 善 (Zen) means good or better. Your daily habits — the small, repeated actions that build the mountain one grain of dust at a time.

Strand Identity

Each strand of your Ryughi carries a kanji that represents its spirit. These are drawn from the default strand library — your own strands may carry different characters.

志 Kokorozashi Ambition, will, or aspiration. Used for Capital and Building strands — the material and financial foundation of your life's work.
史 Shi / Fumi History or chronicle. Used for The Ancient World strands — the study of what came before and the wisdom it carries forward.
鍛 Tan / Kitae To forge or temper. Used for The Warrior's Body strands — physical training, health, and the discipline of the body.
匠 Sho / Takumi Master craftsperson or artisan. Used for Creative Mastery strands — the creative disciplines and skills pursued to a high level.
愛 Ai Love or affection. Used for Faith and Love strands — relationships, connection, and the bonds that give life its deepest meaning.

Milestones of Continuance

Earned by maintaining your daily practice streak. Each stamp is a different colour and character — they appear in your Shokunin calendar as you earn them.

継 Kei — 7 days Continuance. To carry on, to keep going.
錬 Ren — 21 days Tempering. To refine through repeated trial, as metal is worked.
鍛 Tan — 30 days Forging. The heat of the fire is within you.
剛 Gou — 50 days Unyielding. Rigid, hard, unbreakable. The will that does not bend.
士 Shi — 100 days Warrior or samurai. One who has proven themselves through sustained action.
将 Sho — 180 days Commander or general. One who leads — beginning with themselves.
道 Do — 365 days The Way. A full year of unbroken practice. The path has become inseparable from the person walking it.

Interface Symbols

週 Shuu — Week The weekly reflection seal. Appears in red on the Shokunin calendar when a week has been sealed with a reflection. Tap to read it.
月 Tsuki / Getsu — Month The monthly reflection seal. Appears in red beside month names on the Ikigai year calendar when a monthly reflection exists. Tap to read it.
省 Sei / Kaerimi Reflection or introspection. Appears as a cream mark on days in the Shokunin calendar where a daily note was written. Tap the day to read the entry.
印 In / Shirushi Seal or stamp. Appears when a reflection has been completed and sealed — the mark that something has been honoured and closed.
旅 Tabi — Journey Travel or journey. Marks the Travel Mode — a reduced practice set for when you are away from your usual environment.
斬 Zan — To Cut To slash or sever. Appears on the delete confirmation blade — the irreversible action of removing something from your practice.
統計 Toukei — Statistics Statistics or data. Appears on the stats panel showing your completion rates and streak numbers across all practices.

Proverbs

千里の道も一歩から Even a journey of a thousand ri begins with a single step. The proverb beneath the Ryughi section — a reminder that the distance ahead is not the point. The step is.
塵も積もれば山となる Even dust, when accumulated, becomes a mountain. The kaizen proverb — what daily practice actually is. Not grand gestures, but grains of dust laid one at a time.
北斗七星 Hokuto Shichisei — the Big Dipper constellation (literally: North Star Seven Stars). The banner on the welcome screen. In Japanese tradition, a star formation used for navigation — you always know which way is north.

The characters are not decoration.
They are the thing itself.
